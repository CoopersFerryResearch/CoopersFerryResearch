Chapter 3 - Excavation

Cooper’s Ferry is an active archaeological excavation site and is one of the largest and most technologically complex sites in North America. The ultimate goal of Cooper’s Ferry is to excavate and map everything in place to gather data towards identifying and confirming human occupation in this region of the Lower Salmon River Canyon from 7,000-13,500 years in the past.

Every year during the off season, archaeologists completely bury the site to rebuild the local fishing spot, and to deter looters who might otherwise dig into the site illegally. Instead of filling the site with dirt after the field season, all of the dirt that was excavated in the dig season is put into sandbags and these sandbags go back into the hole to replace the volume taken out. Before the site can be uncovered, the Cooper’s Ferry team builds a sun shelter, to protect against UV rays. Once students reach the bottom of the sandbags, they will find notes and diagrams leftover from the previous excavation year that they can use to pick up where the last student left off.

At Cooper’s Ferry the goal is to find everything in the ground exactly as it was placed, as much as possible. Using trowels, wooden skewers, and brushes, archaeologists remove sediment from around objects of a certain size. Anything that is dime-sized or larger is kept in the ground, drawn, and mapped with a Total Station. Once the object, it’s location in space, and its material has been documented, the artifact is ready to be removed and put into an appropriate container to be observed later. At the screening station archaeologists send buckets of sediment through two different sized metal screens to find any small artifacts they might have missed. These artifacts are sorted by type of material and put into small plastic bags to be examined later.
