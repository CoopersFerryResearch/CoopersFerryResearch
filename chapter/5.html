Chapter 5 - Technology

Along with being a flagship excavation site for archaeology in the Pacific Northwest, Cooper’s Ferry is best known for its groundbreaking use of progressive technology and methodology. Dr. Loren Davis and the archaeological team at Cooper’s Ferry actively use an extensive array of different data collection and management techniques to record and catalogue hundreds of thousands of unique artifacts. Many of these methods are pioneered here at Cooper’s Ferry and have been greatly admired by members of the archaeological community.

  • Go Pro Cameras - Time lapse cameras are positioned over the excavation site to review and monitor the excavation. These cameras take a photo every ten seconds, and those pictures can be strung together to recreate what happened at the site that day. Back in the lab, after the excavation has closed, the photos can be reviewed to check for excavation accuracy.
  • Laser Total Station – The Laser Total Station is the most accurate piece of measuring equipment available and is used to keep track of exactly where everything was found. How one piece relates to another is critical in determining its context. The Total Station shoots a laser at a prism, and bounces back to determine the X,Y,Z data; which translates into the known distance between the north wall, the east wall and the actual elevation of the artifact. The artifact is then given an RN, or “Reading Number” so that it can be catalogued and recalled at a later date.
  • Xbox 360 Kinect - Occasionally the Cooper’s Ferry team will use an Xbox 360 ‘Kinect’ scanner to make a 3D scan of the unit level they’re excavating. Cooper’s Ferry uses an open source program to overwrite the Kinect’s normal functions and refocus them towards capturing elevation and color in its scan. Back at the lab, archaeologists can then take this scan and build a 3D model of the site, level by level, for the entire dig site.
  • Archie Archive System Database - Cooper’s Ferry uses a web page based data entry and catalogue system, called ‘Archie’. With Archie, archaeologists at Cooper’s Ferry can link relevant data to artifacts and levels to help best recreate the excavation process back in the lab in Corvallis, Oregon. ‘Archie’ allows users to link photos and field notes to accompany standardized data entry across the site.
  • 3-D Scanning and Printing - Many of the artifacts uncovered at Cooper’s Ferry are fragile, priceless and wind up archived in a box or museum where they cannot be accessed. This was the case at least until Dr. Loren Davis purchased a 3D scanner and 3D printer to transform priceless artifacts into universal teaching tools. Today, the most interesting artifacts are catalogued, scanned and printed to use as teaching tools for classrooms all over the United States.
